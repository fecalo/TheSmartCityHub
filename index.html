About Smart Cities

Smart Cities are technologically modern urban areas that use different types of electronic methods and sensors to collect specific data. Information gained from that data is used to manage assets, resources and services efficiently; in return, that data is used to improve operations across the city. Examples of smart cities include New York City, Barcelona, and London.

Characteristics of Smart Cities

  • Make more efficient use of physical infrastructure (roads, built environment and other physical assets) through artificial intelligence and data analytics in order to support a strong and healthy economic, social, cultural development.
  • Engage effectively with local governance by use of open innovation processes and e-participation, improving the collective intelligence of the city's institutions through e-governance, with emphasis placed on citizen participation and co-design.
  • Learn, adapt and innovate and thereby respond more effectively and promptly to changing circumstances by improving the intelligence of the city.
  • Evolve towards a strong integration of all dimensions of human intelligence, collective intelligence, and also artificial intelligence within the city.
  • Overall better quality of life for residents.

Smart City Technologies

  1. Information and Communication Technology (ICT) - unified communications and the integration of telecommunications (telephone lines and wireless signals) and computers, as well as necessary enterprise software, middleware, storage and audiovisual, that enable users to access, store, transmit, understand and manipulate information.
  2. Internet of Things (IoT) - physical objects (or groups of such objects) with sensors, processing ability, software and other technologies that connect and exchange data with other devices and systems over the Internet or other communications networks.
  3. Smart Grids - an important technology in smart cities. The improved flexibility of the smart grid permits greater penetration of highly variable renewable energy sources such as solar power and wind power.
  4. Mobile Devices - allows citizens to connect to the smart city services.
  5. Smart Homes
  6. Bicycle-Sharing Systems
  7. Smart Mobility
  8. Intelligent Transportation and CCTV Systems
  9. Digital Libraries
  10. Online Collaborative Sensor Data Management Platforms - online database services that allow sensor owners to register and connect their devices to feed data into an online database for storage and allow developers to connect to the database and build their own applications based on that data.
  11. Supporting Technology - include remote work, telehealth, the blockchain, fintech, and online banking technology.
  12. Electronic Cards - possess a unique encrypted identifier that allows the owner to log into a range of government provided services (or e-services) without setting up multiple accounts. The single identifier allows governments to aggregate data about citizens and their preferences to improve the provision of services and to determine common interests of groups.
  13. Retractable Bollards - allow to restrict access inside city centers (i.e. to delivery trucks resupplying outlet stores).
  14. Energy Data Management Systems (EDMS) - help to save cities energy by recording data and using it to increase efficiency.
  15. Cost-Benefit Analysis - assess whether it is economically and ecologically beneficial to implement some technologies at all, and also compare the cost-effectiveness of each technology among each other.

The Frameworks of Smart Cities

  1. Techonology - different combinations of technological infrastructure such as digital, intelligent, ubiquitous, wired, hybrid, and infocity, interact to form the array of smart city technologies with varying levels of interaction between human and technological systems.
  2. Human - the economy, knowledge networks, and human support systems, which in turn, fuel the creativity, learning, knowledge, and humanity of the people.
  3. Institutional - investments in the human/social capital and IT infrastructure that fuel sustainable growth and enhance quality of life, through participatory governance.
  4. Energy - efficient application of integrated energy technologies in the city allowing the development of more self-sustaining areas or even Positive Energy Districts that produce more energy than consume. Electric companies, working partnership with city officials, technology companies and a number of other institutions, play key roles in the development of smart cities.
  5. Data Management - a combination of data collection, processing, and disseminating technologies in conjunction with networking and computing technologies and data security and privacy measures encouraging the application of innovation to promote the overall quality of life for its citizens and covering dimensions that include: utilities, health, transportation, entertainment and government services.
